
# Iron Pilgrim — Worldbuilding & Story

> A broken machine that doesn't remember why, carrying the last human across a world of machines that don't remember why they're fighting.

> [!note] Status
> **Overview.md** is the high-level game summary; this doc is the detailed story spine and is authoritative for narrative. Both are reconciled to the current vision: the mech is an AI (not a fused human pilot), locomotion is a procedural leg rig, and the cargo's nature is defined author-side.

---

## The world

The planet is inhabited almost entirely by **robots** — and a scatter of **strange, half-wrong animals** (see *Creatures*, Overview.md). Humans are gone. The machines have been at **war with each other** for a long time, in factions, for reasons that no longer have a clean answer — not to the player, and not to the combatants. The world is dying around the fighting: exhausted, ruined, slow.

The player never gets a tidy explanation of any of this. They get **the world** — ruins, wreckage, faction markings, recovered data fragments, the behaviour of the things still moving — and they assemble a partial picture. The reveal at the end recontextualises that picture; it does not close it.

### Why the robots fight — *leading candidate, kept ambiguous*

The humans left (or died), and the machines inherited the unfinished argument *about* humans: one faction wanted to preserve or restore them, one wanted them gone, most have degraded past remembering which side they were ever on — and all of them are still shooting. The war is theological and the theology is forgotten.

This is the version that rhymes with everything else, but it stays implied. What matters mechanically is the **consequence**: the player's mech is carrying a literal living human, which makes it a walking answer — or a walking provocation — to the war. Any faction that registers the cargo reacts: protect it, destroy it, or is too far gone to notice. That's the stakes layer under "environmental storytelling."

## The mech — what the player is

The player **is** the mech — not a pilot inside it, *the machine itself*. It is **silicon-based life: an AI**. It is in bad shape, barely functional, and **doesn't fully recall what happened** — to itself, to the world, or to its purpose. The cockpit interior is the character space; the screens flickering back to life as it repairs itself *are* memory returning.

It is **not a war robot and not a piloted mech.** It's a battered **utility / industrial / scout machine** — built to traverse and to *carry*. It has picked up **light, improvised weaponry** along the way because the world made it necessary, not because it was built to fight.

### Shape — *proposal, not locked*

A **biped "porter"**: hunched, load-bearing, the protected core compartment in the chest/belly. Rationale:
- Reads as a *worker*, not a soldier — no humanoid war-mech silhouette.
- Still person-shaped enough that the player reads the mech as a *someone* (it is — it's an AI), which a four-legged hauler would lose.
- The cargo-in-the-chest posture is a gift for the idle animation (it's curled protectively around something) and for the ending (you open the thing you've been built around the whole game).
- Quadruped stays an option only if "low, animal, pack-machine" becomes the desired read — not for the modest IK savings.

### Weaponry

Salvaged and improvised, never a "weapon port" — a repurposed cutting laser, a mining charge, a flare launcher bolted on after something nearly killed it. Each is a real component in the graph (mass, power draw, mounting location, damage propagation). Combat is **rare, ugly, and costly**: scarce ammo/charge, damage that cascades through the component graph, a fight you "won" that still left you limping. "Occasional pew-pew is fun" only survives if it stays a desperate exception, not a loop.

## The cargo — *author-side truth; player must not learn this early*

Inside the mech's core compartment is a **sealed container**. Inside the container is a **human infant** — the last human. The mech's mission is to get it to safety. The mech may not even know *what* it carries — it may know only "cargo: protected, do not open" — which keeps the player and the mech ignorant together.

**The player learns this at the end, by the container being opened.** The whole game is built so the reveal recontextualises the journey — every detour, every repair, every constrained action.

### Reveal discipline

- The container is labelled something flat: `PAYLOAD — SEALED`, never "infant."
- A recurring **unclassifiable signal** comes from it — a faint rhythmic pulse the mech's degraded sensors won't resolve (heartbeat? telemetry? the player decides).
- The mech is **constrained in ways the player feels but isn't told**: actions refused with "cargo integrity" rather than an explanation; losing or damaging the container has real consequences. (This dovetails with Overview.md's "the cargo must be protected.")
- The signal/north-star (Overview.md) can stay as the destination device: a direction → a shape → almost a voice → the place the cargo is meant to go (a vault, a haven, another preservationist machine — *open*).
- **The ending question, deliberately unresolved here:** does the mech survive to hand the child over, or is the last act the mech shutting down with the container safe? Is the child still alive after a journey this long? "Bleak, slow, lonely" suggests the answer isn't a clean rescue. Decide late.

## Memory & amnesia — what it gates

Be precise or it goes soft. The mech remembers **procedures** (how to walk, repair, navigate) but not **meaning** (who built it, why, what the cargo is, what happened to the world). The repair loop and the memory loop are **the same loop**: restoring the data-core components the component graph already models gives back fragments — and fragments are also how the world's history surfaces. Amnesia is the in-fiction reason the player starts ignorant and the diegetic reason the screens start dark.

## Other inhabitants

- **Robot factions** — the warring machines. Markings, wreckage, patrol behaviour, the occasional still-functioning unit. Hostile, indifferent, or (rarely, dangerously) *interested in the cargo*. Mostly the player avoids, hides, or — last resort — fights.
- **Strange animals** — the half-wrong creatures of Overview.md's *Creatures* section. Some gigantic. Nothing friendly; the mech is an intruder. They have nothing to do with the war — they just live here, which makes the world feel like more than a battlefield.

## How the story is delivered

1. **The world itself** — ruins, faction debris, the dying landscape, what's still moving and how it behaves.
2. **Recovered data fragments** — surfaced through repair (memory) and scavenging (history). Same system, two faces.
3. **The mech's own constraints** — what it refuses to do, and why it won't say.
4. **The container** — its label, its pulse, the way the mech is built around it.
5. **The final reveal** — the container opens. Everything before it re-reads. More questions than answers, but the right kind.

No dialogue. No exposition dumps. No HUD telling the player what to feel.
